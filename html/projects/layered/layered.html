Computational Geometries

Monte Carlo (MC) radiation transport calculations are used to assess the safety and performance of nuclear reactors and other nuclear systems. The MC method uses a random walk technique to simulate the paths that particles (i.e., neutrons and/or photons) take as they move through a computational geometry representating the system. By simulating a large number of particles, statistical methods can be used to estimate radiological quantities such as particle flux, biological dose rate, and the neutron multiplication factor (which determines if a reactor is subcritical, critical, or supercritical).

MC codes track of positions and directions of particles within a computational geometry using ray tracing, the same algorithm for rendering 3D graphics. The implementation of the ray tracing algorithm depends on how the computational geometry is specificed. Historically, MC codes have supported computational geometries specified in Combinatorial Solid Geometry (CSG) formats. With CSG, the boundaries of geometry cells are specified geometric primitives (e.g., planes, cuboids, and spheres) in a text-based format. These primatives can be combined to form more complex shapes. In addition, support for Computer-Aided Design (CAD) computational geometries has been added more recently to most production-level MC codes.
